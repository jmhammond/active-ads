Question 5.1.1. Response Question.

In mathematics, the word partition is used in two contexts. One is for partitions of sets, as described in Section 2.3. The other is for partitions of a positive integer. An example of a partition of \(5\) is \(3+1+1\text{,}\) a sum of positive integers equal to \(5\text{.}\) It is customary to write the terms of the sum in non-increasing order since \(1+3+1\) is considered the same partition of \(5\text{.}\) The other partitions of 5 are \(5\text{,}\) \(4+1\text{,}\) \(3+2\text{,}\) \(2+2+1\text{,}\) \(2+1+1+1\text{,}\) and \(1+1+1+1+1\text{.}\) How might a listing of all partitions of an integer like \(5\) help in listing all partitions of a set with that many elements?
